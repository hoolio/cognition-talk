# Talk Outlines — The Industrialization of Cognition

**Style note.** Each version uses the same five-act spine. Each slide carries at most one sentence. Cadence is step-by-step: the speaker advances each click; each slide does one beat of work. Big graphics anchor section openings and key turns. Where you see `[GRAPHIC: …]`, that's a full-bleed visual moment — no sentence, the slide *is* the graphic.

**The five-act spine** (constant across versions; only depth varies)

| Act | Function | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| I — THE GROUND | Acknowledge what is already said | Disarms the audience; signals you've read the room |
| II — THE EMERGING | Acknowledge what is half-said | Signals you've read the frontier |
| III — THE SYNTHESIS | Land the original framing | Industrialization of cognition; the moment of translation |
| IV — THE HEURISTIC | Convert framing into a tool | Seven-factor matrix; three pathways |
| V — THE APPLICATION | Apply tool to reality | Categories, verticals, the nine, hot takes, white space |

**The fresh / semi-fresh / not-fresh map** (the architectural insight that organizes Acts I–III)

```
NOT FRESH (everyone says this)            SEMI-FRESH (some people say this)        FRESH (this synthesis)

AI affects white-collar work       →      epistemic outsourcing              →     industrialization of cognition
AI is a general-purpose tech       →      distributed cognition              →     where value accrues, structurally
AI may displace cognitive labor    →      collapse of ideation cost          →     matrix predicting winners vs losers
knowledge may become abundant      →      AI-native workflows                →     anti–black box as investable layer
                                                                                    moment of translation as capture point
```

## How the four versions are designed to compose

The four outlines share spine and visuals; only depth differs.

The 5-min is the synthesis only — drop into Act III, land on V's white space.
The 15-min compresses Acts I and II into single-wave acknowledgments; develops III, IV, V.
The 30-min is the canonical talk; every act gets its full sentence-rhythm.
The 45-min is the keynote; adds the hot-takes pass, deeper verticals, more breathing room around the moment of translation.

Promote/demote slides between versions by reusing the same text. The pin-factory beat (S34–S41 in 45-min) collapses to a single graphic in 30-min and disappears in 15-min. The hot takes (S115–S120) are an additive section that only appears in 45-min and could appear as one slide in 30-min.

## What I'd want to confirm before drafting any of these as full decks

Three choices to make before I commit to a deck:

The graphic style. Lessig-style (very minimal, near-text-only), Bret-Victor-style (rich custom diagrams, hand-drawn feel), or something in between? Determines how much visual asset work to budget.

The pin-factory beat. Keep it in the longer versions or strip it out? It's powerful in 45-min, fine in 30-min, marginal in 15-min, dead weight in 5-min. Current outline assumes drop-it-as-we-shorten.

The hot takes. Bundle into one slide or run as a sequence? In 45-min the sequence works (S115–S120). In 30-min, one slide listing four takes is probably better. Confirm preference.

Once those three are settled I can build any of the four versions to a finished deck.
